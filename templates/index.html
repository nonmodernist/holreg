From 1910 to 1961, Hollywood made more than 100 films inspired by the novels and short stories of American women regionalists—illuminating the landscapes, cultures, and social debates that shaped a nation.

Hollywood Regionalism explores the US film industry's fascination with American women regionalists both famous and forgotten.

While academic tradition limits regionalism to a brief literary movement (1880s-1910), Hollywood reveals a different story. For over fifty years, filmmakers transformed these regional works into vivid screen adaptations, bringing their stories to national audiences.

This remarkable half-century of adaptations demonstrates how regionalist storytelling evolved across media—from magazine pages to movie screens—and continued to resonate with audiences long after scholars declared the movement finished.

About This Project

Hollywood Regionalism is about an overlooked chapter in American cultural history: how women's regional literature became national cinema across five decades.

The site's mission is to build a resource that:

  • Documents these significant but understudied film adaptations
  • Connects researchers, students, and film enthusiasts with viewing opportunities
  • Reveals how regional stories found their way to national audiences
  • Expands our understanding of both literary regionalism and early Hollywood

By preserving and examining this cultural legacy, Hollywood Regionalism illuminates how diverse American voices and experiences were translated from page to screen during a pivotal era in both literature and filmmaking.
